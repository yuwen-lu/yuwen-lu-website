Machine Learning-Powered

Our app enabled goalkeepers to record their training videos and replay them, so that they can detect flaws in their practices from an objective view. We also offered personalized training plans for users. More than that, we integrated Machine Learning classification to detect the goalkeepers' performed training tasks (left/right dive, jump catch, etc.) and automatically calculate the completion status of their training plans. In the future, we can build other machine learning models to analyse the users' performance such as dive distance and jump height, to offer more professional feedback for their training.

As one developer in the team, I contributed to building the app using Swift and integrated the newly-released Apple ARKit motion detection module into our machine learning pipeline. I integrated the module to detect human's joint positions in the video frame and fed the data to our LSTM-based model, which conducted the classification.

Your Personal Card Manager for Payment

Imagine you have several bank cards and you are paying for the dinner with your crush: American Express has a higher return rate, but Bank of American has a promotion activity with this restaurant... "Cell in CELL"

Using DNA Hydrogels to Capture Circulating Tumor Cells

Circulating Tumor Cells (CTCs) are critical cells in cancer patients' blood. With living CTCs, medical experts can understand patients' cancer situation better and provide more grounded treatments. However, many current seperation methods for CTCs cannot ensure the activity and intactness of the cells.

In our project, we used DNA Hydrogels to form a "prison cell" on the surface of CTCs to protect and seperate them from blood samples without damage. It was an efficient and economic method and provided solid foundation for future medical research. We named our project "Cell in CELL" to vividly represent the CTCs in the DNA Hydrogel "prison cells".

Human Practices

What makes iGEM different is its focus on the projects' social impact. As science and engineering students, we often neglect our technical solutions' social influences; iGEM encourages us to explore the intersection by setting requirements on Human Practices, where we visit biochemical companies and researchers, go to local schools and communities to expand our projects' social benefits.

In our project, our team got engaged with our local community in a variety of ways. 2019 happened to be the 70th anniversary of our university. During the carnival celebrations, we set up an information booth where we introduce our project to people and answer their questions and concerns. Considering the general public's concern over chemical and biological research, we invited our university's debate team, a national championship winner, to have a public debate over whether biological and chemical research has positive or negative effects on our world. Debaters from the two sides illustrated both the benefits and risks for conducting research in the related fields. We concluded that though the risks are real, as long as we have proper regulations and the researchers follow moral ethics, the research work can push forward human's knowledge boundaries and contribute to solutions that benefit all mankind. In addition, we also went to local high schools to deliver lectures on SynthBio and invited the students to our lab to conduct experiments themselves. We believed that it could help those interested find their enthusiasm in STEM earlier in their lives.

Please refer to our wiki page on Human Practices for more details.
